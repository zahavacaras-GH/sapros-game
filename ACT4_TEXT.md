I opened the first.

The man was named Edrik Vant. I had never heard of him. He was a logistics coordinator for a Quadrant transit company. He had been flagged for *facilitation of association* — meaning he had, as part of his job, scheduled a transit corridor that had ended up being used by a group the Council was watching. There was a checkbox on the form. The checkbox was at the bottom. The checkbox said *EXECUTE.*

I checked the box. I signed. I moved to the next.

The second was a woman in her thirties. She had hosted a discussion group like the one Ennis Ardin had hosted, before me, in another Quadrant. I had never killed her predecessor. I had let him go. Edge cases. I checked the box. I signed.

She had been flagged for *known association* — she had, sometime in the last year, attended a single meeting of a Quadrant labor group. The group had been small. The meeting had been one of three.

The investiture was held in the white room.

The white room is on the top floor of the Hetairoi tower. It is round. The ceiling is high. The walls are bare. It has the acoustics of a chapel, though it is not a chapel. There are no chairs except a single low one in the center. The candidate sits in the chair. The Hetairoi stand in a ring around the candidate. They place a small silver pin on the candidate's collar, one at a time, until all five pins are in place.

I sat in the chair.

The pins went on. Each Hetairoi, in turn, said the small ritual phrase. *Service is privilege. Privilege is service.* The phrase had been said over me five times, now, at five investitures. I had stopped hearing the words years ago.

Vespera was last. Her pin was the smallest. Her hands were warm. She placed it on my collar with a precision I had not expected — she had done this many times, and she had not gotten bored of it.

She leaned in. She said, in a voice only I could hear, "Welcome home."

I rose.

There was a mirror at the back of the room. I had not noticed it, going in. It was not a *prominent* mirror. It was tall and narrow and set into the wall in a way that, from most angles, made it look like a door. I caught my reflection in it as I turned.

I was nineteen.

The face in the mirror was not the face of a nineteen-year-old. It had the right number of years. The wear was wrong. The eyes had four extra years on them, at least, and the mouth had something I had not seen before — a small set to the corners that made the mouth look like it was *deciding* something at all times, even when it was at rest.

The mouth had not been like that, four years before, in a hallway, holding a marker.

I had become the kind of man whose face was always deciding.

I would think, later, that the white room had been built precisely so that the candidate would catch this reflection. That the mirror was the *point* of the room. That the Hetairoi placed five pins one at a time so that the candidate would have time, slowly, to recognize the new face.

It was the kind of design Vespera would have made.

I had no proof. I did not need proof.
