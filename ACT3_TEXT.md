## NODE `t3.1` — Jason's Office

**Image:** `t3.1_jasons_office.webp`

Jason's office was on the fourth floor of the Achaean wing, in a corner with two windows. The Capitol lights came in at a low angle in the evenings and made the whole room the color of old paper.

He had two armchairs. He had a kettle on a hot plate. He had a bookshelf that I would later realize was curated — not for show, but with the precision of a man who had been thinking for a long time about what was on it. On the lowest shelf, in a worn leather binding, was a copy of *Meditations*. He took it down on my first night.

"Read this," he said. "The job is hard. This helps."

He poured me tea. Black tea, the kind that tasted like wood. He didn't ask if I wanted it.

"How's your family?" he said.

I had been expecting a debrief. I had been expecting strategy. I had been expecting the kind of opening conversation a senior officer has with his new subordinate. I had not been expecting *how's your family,* delivered like he'd known me twenty years.

I was at the desk now. The man behind the desk was the man who had nodded at me from the back of two ceremonies. He had been watching me for two years.

I had to choose how to answer.

**Choices:**
- **Tell him about Bran. Be honest.** → `t3.1_open`
- **Deflect — politely.** → `t3.1_deflect`
- **Lie. Invent a tragic backstory.** → `t3.1_lie`

### Sub-node `t3.1_open`

I told him about Bran.

I told him the way I'd told Sophie — the fractions, the rice, the bright thing he was learning not to do. I didn't know why I was telling him. I had decided, on the way up the stairs, to be careful. Then I'd sat down and my mouth had done something else.

He listened the way he poured tea. Steady. Unhurried. As if listening were a discipline like marksmanship and he had been practicing it longer than I had been alive.

When I was done he said, "He sounds like a good kid."

"He is."

"Don't bring him here. Not for visits. Not for anything. The kindest thing you can do for a sibling, in this work, is to keep them in a different version of your life."

He said it without any weight. The way you'd tell someone to wear a coat in the rain.

I drank the tea. I knew, drinking it, that I had just given a man I did not know enough about my brother for him to find Bran inside of an hour.

I knew that. I drank the tea anyway.

That was a new kind of knowing.

## NODE `t3.3a` — Conversations with Jason

**Image:** `t3.3a_jason_walk.webp`

---

We walked, mostly. He wasn't a desk talker. He liked the high balcony on the east wing where you could see the city spread out below — the Quadrants in the distance, the wall, the lights of the third Quadrant where Quinn was still in a holding cell.

He talked about everything except the work, until he was ready to talk about the work.

When he was ready, it was on the third walk. We were standing at the rail. He said, "What do you think the Council is for, Mika?"

I had answers. I had practiced answers. I had been practicing answers for two years.

He held up a hand before I started.

"I'm not asking what you think the right answer is. I'm asking what you actually think."

I told him I thought it was for control.

He nodded. He didn't disagree. He said, "Control of what?"

"Of people," I said.

"That's a lazy answer. Control of *what about* people?"

I didn't have a fast answer.

He waited. Then he said, "The Council exists to keep eight million people from killing each other. The thing it controls is the *appetite for chaos.* The chaos is real. The appetite is real. If you ever come to think the Council is the bad guy, ask yourself who keeps the bakery open. Ask yourself who makes sure the trains run. Ask yourself why your brother is alive."

He looked at me. He let the silence do the heavy lifting.

He said, "I used to think the way you think. I'm not insulting you. I'm telling you it's a phase. Most people grow out of it by twenty. The smart ones grow out of it earlier."

The wind came off the city. It was warm. It smelled like rain.

I had to decide what to say back.

Jason looked at me.

I noticed, in other words, that I had won the argument and that I had also, somehow, lost.

## NODE `t3.3b` — First Assassination

**Image:** `t3.3b_assassination.webp`

---

The target was a forty-year-old man named Ennis Ardin. He taught literature at a high school in the second Quadrant. He had hosted three private discussion groups in the last year about Council policy. The groups were small. Six people. They met in his living room.

He was not a leader of anything. He was a man who had read books and had opinions and had wanted to talk to other people who had read books and had opinions. The AI had flagged the gatherings as *pre-emergent dissident formation.* The Council had upgraded the flag to *active.* The active flag had landed on my desk.

He had a wife and a five-year-old daughter. They were sleeping in the next room.

I stood outside his apartment door at three in the morning with a lockpick kit and a service pistol with a suppressor screwed onto the barrel. The corridor was empty. The lock was a standard residential.

I had four options.

**Choices:**
- **Clean kill — do it right, get out.** → `t3.3b_clean`
- **Messy kill — make it look like a robbery.** → `t3.3b_messy`
- **Fake the death — let him escape, file the report.** → `t3.3b_fake`
- **Refuse. Abort the operation.** → `t3.3b_abort`

---

### Sub-node `t3.3b_clean`

I went in.

He was sleeping on his side. I checked the room first. Nothing on the nightstand worth taking, no security, no surprises. I took two steps to the bed. I waited until he turned, which men sleeping on their sides do every few minutes, and the turn put him face-up.

The shot was clean. The suppressor did its work.

I was out of the building in eleven minutes.

The wife found him at six.

I filed the report. The report said *target neutralized, no collateral.* Jason signed it. He didn't look up. He said, "Good work."

I went back to my quarters. I slept four hours.

I noticed, in the morning, that I was not different. I had killed a man — the second man, technically, the first having been the runner at the checkpoint — and I had thought that this kill, the deliberate one, the planned one, would change me visibly. I had been ready to find a different face in the mirror.

The face was the same.

That was the thing that worried me. Not the kill. The sameness.

### Sub-node `t3.3b_abort`

I didn't go in.

That was new information. I filed it away.

The note said an address and a time. The address was a Capitol bar I had never been to. The time was eleven at night.

Kaden looked worse than I had expected.

He had been promoted to Achaean too. Same year. We had not crossed paths, because he had been assigned to a different branch — internal ops, the kind that handled Knights who had become problems for other Knights. I had heard he was good at it. Several people had said so, in the slightly impressed, slightly nervous way people talk about a Knight whose work they will never see.

He was thinner. Hollow under the eyes. The boy from the mess hall had been *worn.* This man was *cracked.* You could see, behind the eyes, the place where the cracks had started to spread.

But the mind was sharp. The mind was, if anything, sharper.

He bought me a drink. He didn't drink one himself.

He said, "I have a plan, Just Mika. I want to tell it to you. It's going to take five years. It's going to require both of us to stay alive, which is the unpleasant part. And it's going to put us, by the time we're twenty-two, in a position to kill a Hetairoi and replace them."

He smiled a little. The smile didn't reach the rest of his face. The rest of his face hadn't moved, by then, in months.

"Tell me you're listening," he said.

I had to choose what kind of conversation we were going to have.
