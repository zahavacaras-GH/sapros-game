## NODE `t2.1` — Promotion / Meet Sophie

**Image:** `t2.1_promotion_meet_sophie.webp`

---

The Skiritai investiture was held in a small plaza inside the Capitol wall, which I had now seen the inside of so many times it had stopped feeling like a wall. Eighteen of us. Black uniforms. Silver shoulder insignia.

The girl beside me was laughing.

She was laughing at something a senior had said while he was pinning her insignia, and the laugh was loud and unselfconscious and it had the particular quality of a laugh that had never been made to be quiet. I had grown up in apartments where laughter was something you kept under a hand. This laughter belonged in rooms with high ceilings.

She was tall. Pale. Long platinum hair in a high braid. The uniform fit her like it had been made for her, which, looking at the cut, it probably had.

She caught me staring and didn't pretend not to notice. "You look terrified," she said.

"I'm fine."

"You look terrified, but in a polite way. I'm Sophie."

"Mika."

"Mika what?"

"Just Mika."

She looked at me a second too long, then smiled like she'd decided something. "Okay, Just Mika. My father's a Hetairoi, so I'm going to be the worst person you've ever met, and I'm telling you that up front so you don't have to figure it out."

I almost laughed.

It was the first time in a year that something had almost gotten a real laugh out of me.

## NODE `t2.2` — Skiritai Hub

The Skiritai station was a different building than the Thebes barracks — newer, cleaner, more glass. The locker room had a window that looked out over a section of the city I had never been able to see, full of buildings I didn't know the names of. We were given a lot more freedom now, which meant a lot more rope.

I had three things I was thinking about.

Sophie was in the next row of lockers, telling somebody a story about a horse. I could spend more time with her. There was no harm in it. (*There was harm in it. I knew there was harm in it. I told myself there wasn't.*)

I had Skiritai-level access to records now. Not full access — that was Achaean — but enough to find Quinn's file if I was careful. I could spend an evening with my head in the system and learn something real.

And I hadn't seen Niko in a year. He was still in the old neighborhood. Last I'd heard, he was driving a delivery cart for a bakery and having opinions about the Council nobody had asked for.

Three things. A month off rotation. I had to choose.

## NODE `t2.3a` — Build with Sophie

**Image:** `t2.3a_sophie_training.webp`

---

She was in the gym every morning at six. So was I. We had not planned this. It happened the way friendship happens between two people who are bad at admitting they want it.

We trained. We sparred. She was stronger than me and I was faster than her, and we landed in a rhythm where neither of us could win cleanly, and we both, for the first time in our lives, found it pleasant to lose to someone.

She told me about her family. Her father was on the Council — *the food and resources committee*, she said, like she was apologizing for it being that one and not a more glamorous one. Her mother had died when she was nine. She had three older brothers, all of them in the military somewhere. Her father had wanted a fourth son. He'd gotten Sophie instead. *I think it's worked out for him*, she said.

She didn't ask about my family. Not for two weeks. When she finally did, she did it the right way — at the end of a sparring session, both of us flat on our backs on the mat, breathing hard, looking at the ceiling.

"What's your family like, Just Mika?"

I had practiced an answer. I had several practiced answers. I had a whole rotation.

I had to choose which version of myself to give her.

**Choices:**
- **Tell her something real.** → `t2.3a_real`
- **Give her the version she wants.** → `t2.3a_perform`

### Sub-node `t2.3a_real`

I told her about Bran.

Not the rest of it — not Quinn, not the ultimatum, not the mother who didn't know I was a Knight and wouldn't be told until she was. But Bran I gave her. I told her about the fractions. I told her about the rice he pushed across the table. I told her about the way he'd learned that bright was dangerous.

She didn't say anything for a long time.

Then she said, "You should have him visit. The Knights have family weekends. They're horrible. Everyone pretends. But you should do it anyway."

I told her I'd think about it.

I knew I wouldn't. The system would learn his name.

But I'd given her something true, and she'd held it carefully, and she'd given me something back: the knowledge that she could hold things.

It was the kind of thing I would not have understood was valuable, a year before.

## NODE `t2.3b` — Investigate Quinn (Deeper)

**Image:** `t2.3b_records_skiritai.webp`

---

Skiritai access wasn't full access. I couldn't pull a sealed file or close one or move one. I could *read* most things, with restrictions, and I could read them after midnight, when the duty officer was usually asleep at the desk.

It took me four nights to find Quinn.

He was alive. He was in a low-security holding facility in the third Quadrant. His case had been *open and pending review* for fifteen months — meaning the Council had not decided what to do with him and was in no hurry. They were waiting to see if the underground he'd allegedly tried to help showed any sign of being worth the leverage. If it didn't, they'd close his file. *Close* was a word I'd learned, in this place, had two meanings.

I read his physical description. He was thinner. They had shaved his head.

I had two options.

**Choices:**
- **Try to pass him a message.** → `t2.3b_message`
- **Note the file's location, leave it alone, save it for later.** → `t2.3b_note`

---

### Sub-node `t2.3b_message`

I used a Knights' courier line. I encrypted it badly on purpose — a low-grade encryption that would look like an ex-girlfriend's idiot kid brother had sent it, not a Skiritai. The message was three words.

*Still here. Holding.*

He would know who it was. He'd been the only person I'd ever told about my private notation system, the one I used for math homework. Three words in that notation, on a low-grade encryption layer, was unmistakably me.

It was also unmistakably *traceable*, if anyone in the system cared to look. The terminal was registered to my name.

I told myself the risk was acceptable.

I told myself the risk was acceptable a lot, that year. The phrase had a kind of warmth to it. *I told myself the risk was acceptable* meant: *I am the kind of person who weighs risks now.*

## NODE `t2.3c` — Visit Niko

**Image:** `t2.3c_niko.webp`

---

Niko picked the alley. He did not invite me to the bakery and he did not come to the Capitol. He picked an alley three blocks from his apartment, at eleven at night, and when he saw me coming he didn't smile.

He was taller than I remembered. Or I'd gotten shorter. Both, probably.

"You came."

"You said to come."

"I said it like a test. Some Knights wouldn't bother."

I didn't have an answer to that. He hadn't said it like a test. We both knew he hadn't said it like a test. He was performing the new shape of our friendship in real time and I was supposed to figure it out.

He said, "Where's Quinn?"

That was the whole conversation. He had not asked how I was. He had not asked about training. He had not asked, the way I had thought he might, whether I was okay. He'd asked one thing.

I had to choose what to say.

### Sub-node `t2.3c_lie`

"I don't know," I said. "I haven't heard. I can't ask. They watch us."

He looked at me for a long time.

"Mika," he said. "When did you get good at lying?"

He said, "Get out of my city."

He left first. I waited five minutes, then walked back.

I was already composing, in my head, the next three lies I would have to tell him if he ever spoke to me again.

I noticed, walking, that I was good at it. I noticed I felt nothing about being good at it. I noticed that *noticing nothing* had become something I noticed.

## NODE `t2.4` — Forced Beat: First Detention

**Image:** `t2.4_detention.webp`

---

The man's name was Andrei Kos. He was forty-three. He was a high school chemistry teacher.

He had been flagged by the AI for "associative dissent" — meaning he had eaten dinner three times in the last six months with a man who had been flagged for *direct dissent*. The direct dissenter, as far as I could tell from the file, had once written an opinion piece about Council water policy. The opinion piece had not even been published.

We took Kos at his apartment, at nine in the evening, in front of his children. He had two of them. A daughter, ten. A son, six. They were in their pajamas. The son had Knights stickers on his pajamas. He pointed at our uniforms and said, *"They're like in my book,"* and the daughter looked at her father's face and started to cry before anything had happened, because she was ten and she had figured out, faster than her brother, what kind of book it was actually like.

Kos was quiet at first. Then, when the senior Skiritai started reading the warrant, he was less quiet. He begged. He did the begging cleanly — no dignity stuff, no *I demand a lawyer*, no *you're making a mistake*. Just *please. Please. The kids.*

I had to put a hand on his shoulder. Then both hands. Then my arms around him, when he tried to drop to the floor.

He weighed a lot less than I'd expected.

I kept my face arranged in the shape we'd been told to keep our faces in. Calm. Professional. The shape was easy. I had been practicing.

In the van, on the way back, the senior Skiritai looked at me and said, "First one?"

I said, "Yes, sir."

He said, "You'll get used to it."

I noticed he had used the exact phrase the Knight in the car had not used, two years before, when I was the one in the back seat.

I noticed I was now on the other side of that sentence.

## NODE `t2.5` — Climax: Fire on the Dissenter

A week later. A checkpoint at the south end of the Capitol wall. A young man, my age, ran.

He shouldn't have run. The checkpoint was routine. Whatever he was carrying, the AI had only flagged him as *probable* — not certain — and the worst that happens to a probable is a forty-minute interview and then they send you home. He'd run anyway. People do, sometimes. The body does what the body does.

(*Conditional — if `survived_quinn = true` and `relationship_quinn ≥ 5`:*)
*For one second, looking at the back of him as he ran — same height, same lankiness, same way the elbows pumped — I thought he was Quinn. He wasn't. Quinn was three Quadrants away in a holding cell with a shaved head. But the second was there, and it was a long second, and it stained everything that came after it.*

The senior Skiritai — the same one who'd told me I'd get used to it — yelled my name.

"Mika! Drop him!"

The runner was forty meters out and accelerating.

I had a service rifle. It was not a difficult shot.

Cleanup arrived. We filed the report. The CO walked me back to the station and bought me dinner, which was the protocol — first kills got dinner, the way first periods got chocolate, in some other version of childhood that wasn't mine.

I ate the dinner. I slept that night. I was surprised at how well I slept.

That was the worst part.

## NODE `t2.6` — Memory: Walking Home with Bran

**Image:** `t2.6_memory_walking.webp` *(sepia)*

---

I was twelve. Bran was nine.

We were walking home from school, and Bran was eating one of those pickled things from the corner stand, the ones Mom hated because they made his uniform smell. I was making fun of him about it. He was laughing in the way nine-year-olds laugh when they're trying to chew and laugh at the same time, badly.

The afternoon light was gold. The street was warm. There was a woman at the second-floor window of the green building hanging laundry, and the laundry caught the light, and a dog crossed in front of us and Bran tried to pet it and it went past, and we got to the corner of our street and Bran said something about a cartoon he'd seen, and I said something dismissive about the cartoon, and he laughed at me for being dismissive about a cartoon I had not even watched, and we went inside, and that was the day.

That was the whole day.

It came back to me in the van after the shooting. The whole afternoon. In one piece. Like a photograph someone slid under a door.

I sat in the van and I thought about the woman with the laundry, and I thought about the dog, and I thought about Bran trying to laugh and chew at the same time, and I did not cry, because I had stopped crying eight months ago, but I had a feeling in my chest like crying, which was perhaps a thing I would have to learn to live with.

## NODE `t2.7` — Promotion to Achaean

**Image:** `t2.7_promotion_achaean.webp`

---

A year. Then most of another year.

I will not bore us both with the year. There were more checkpoints. There were more reports. There were people I detained whose names I have not chosen to remember. There were three more people I shot, two of whom died, and one of whom didn't, and the one who didn't was a woman in her fifties who I think about more often than the two who did.

Sophie and I trained. Sophie and I ate. Sophie and I became close in the slow particular way of people who have agreed not to talk about the work. We did not talk about the work. We did not have to. The work was something we both did and both were good at and neither of us, by the end of that year, was the same kind of person we'd been at the start.

She told me once, drunk, that her father had said I was *promising*. She'd said it like she was warning me.

I'd thanked her for the warning.

The promotion came in the fall. Achaean black-and-gold. The tailor had measured me a week before and the uniform fit. The sidearm I now knew how to use. There was a small ceremony. There was applause that didn't touch the walls.

The Achaean who would be my squad leader stepped out of the row at the back to shake my hand.

He had pale eyes and a burn scar that started under his jaw and went down into his collar.

I had seen him before. Twice. Once in the gym, when I was fifteen. Once at the back of my Skiritai investiture, watching me, nodding once.

He shook my hand. He held it a beat longer than necessary.

He said, "Welcome. I'm Jason."

I had been waiting two years to hear him say his name.

I had not known I was waiting until I heard it.
